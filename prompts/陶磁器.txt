-- KILN DICTIONARY (35 kilns + 4 modern brands) --

Six Ancient Kilns (六古窯 — Japan Heritage Certified, Fact):
- Shigaraki Ware (信楽焼) — Shiga; natural ash glaze, hi-iro fire marks, organic forms; Tanuki figurines famous
- Bizen Ware (備前焼) — Okayama; unglazed yakishime; hi-iro flame / goma sesame ash / hi-tasuku cord marks / botamochi ash blobs
- Tamba Ware / Tamba-Tachikui Ware (丹波焼) — Hyogo; natural ash glaze, bold flame patterns, folk craft character
- Echizen Ware (越前焼) — Fukui; rough powerful stoneware; natural ash glaze in olive and brown
- Seto Ware (瀬戸焼) — Aichi; Japan's most prolific kiln; "Setomono" became generic term for Japanese pottery
- Tokoname Ware (常滑焼) — Aichi; unglazed red stoneware; famous for kyusu teapots

Traditional kilns:
- Arita Ware (有田焼) — Saga; Japan's first true porcelain (1616); home of Kakiemon and Nabeshima styles
- Imari Ware / Ko-Imari (伊万里焼 / 古伊万里) — Saga; Arita porcelain exported via Imari port; Ko-Imari = pre-1780 export ware
- Kutani Ware (九谷焼) — Ishikawa; bold polychrome overglaze enamel; "five colors"; Ko-Kutani vs. Sai-Kutani
- Hagi Ware (萩焼) — Yamaguchi; porous stoneware; milky glaze over pinkish clay; "nanabake" seven-transformation color change
- Mino Ware (美濃焼) — Gifu; encompasses Shino / Oribe / Ki-Seto / Setoguro; largest ceramics region in Japan
- Karatsu Ware (唐津焼) — Saga; medieval Korean-influenced; varieties: E-Karatsu / Madara-Karatsu / Chosen-Karatsu
- Kyo Ware (京焼) — Kyoto; aristocratic, technically diverse; hub of polychrome enamel development
- Kiyomizu Ware (清水焼) — Kyoto; subtype of Kyo Ware from Kiyomizuzaka area; refined diverse glazes
- Satsuma Ware (薩摩焼) — Kagoshima; White Satsuma (cream crackle glaze, moriage, Kyoto Satsuma export); Black Satsuma (daily use dark clay)
- Mashiko Ware (益子焼) — Tochigi; Mingei folk craft center; earthy iron-brown glazes; Hamada Shoji association
- Raku Ware (楽焼) — Kyoto; hand-built tebineri; slow-fired; Black Raku and Red Raku; Raku family tradition since 16th century
- Nabeshima Ware (鍋島焼) — Saga; formal clan porcelain for shogunate gifts; extremely refined overglaze enamels
- Kakiemon Style (柿右衛門様式) — Saga/Arita; asymmetric design on milky nigoshide porcelain; hugely influential on Meissen and Delft

Regional kilns:
- Tobe Ware (砥部焼) — Ehime; thick white porcelain with indigo-blue hand-painted patterns; uses local Iyo stone
- Mikawachi Ware (三川内焼) — Nagasaki; refined white porcelain; intricate openwork sukashibori; formerly Hirado domain
- Hasami Ware (波佐見焼) — Nagasaki; mass-produced everyday porcelain; contemporary design reputation
- Ohi Ware (大樋焼) — Kanazawa; amber ohi-glaze; hand-built forms for Urasenke tea ceremony; Ohi family since 17th century
- Kasama Ware (笠間焼) — Ibaraki; free-style contemporary pottery tradition; diverse individual potters
- Iga Ware (伊賀焼) — Mie; high-fire stoneware; dramatic natural ash effects; deliberate scorching koge; Momoyama wabi wares
- Izushi Ware (出石焼) — Hyogo; pure white porcelain; extremely thin walls; delicate carving and relief
- Agano Ware (上野焼) — Fukuoka; Korean-influenced tea ceremony ware; Hosokawa clan patronage
- Takatori Ware (高取焼) — Fukuoka; refined thin-walled stoneware; layered transparent glazes; Korean potters origin
- Onta Ware (小鹿田焼) — Oita; living Mingei tradition; water-powered stamps; tobikanna chattering tool marks; UNESCO recognized
- Banko Ware (万古焼) — Mie; fine-grained unglazed kyusu teapots; Yi-xing style red clay; since 18th century

Mino sub-styles:
- Ki-Seto (黄瀬戸) — Yellow Seto; olive-yellow feldspathic glaze with iron-brown brush marks; Momoyama Mino ware
- Nezumi-Shino (鼠志野) — Gray Shino; iron slip under white shino glaze; incised decoration; rarer than standard Shino
- E-Karatsu (絵唐津) — Painted Karatsu; iron-oxide brushwork under translucent ash glaze; grasses and plants motifs
- Madara-Karatsu (斑唐津) — Blotched Karatsu; white milky glaze over dark clay creating marbled effect
- Chosen-Karatsu (朝鮮唐津) — Korean-Karatsu; black and white contrasting glazes on single piece

Modern brands (GPT-5 H-2 addition):
- Noritake (ノリタケ) — Nagoya; fine bone china and export tableware; founded 1904; 870 routing
- Fukagawa Seiji (深川製磁) — Arita; refined porcelain for Imperial household use; 870 routing
- Koransha (香蘭社) — Arita; formal porcelain; century-long export tradition; 870 routing
- Tachikichi (たち吉) — Kyoto; traditional tableware; century-old Kyoto retailer; 870 routing

- Tomei-yu (透明釉) — Clear transparent glaze; allows clay body and underglaze to show
- Shitto-yu (失透釉) — Opaque matte glaze; tin or titanium creates opacity
- Hakeme (刷毛目) — Wide-brush white slip application; brush marks remain visible; Korean origin
- Hagi-yu (萩釉) — Milky semi-translucent; crazing (kan-nyu); porous; develops patina with tea use
- Jun-yu (均釉) — Opalescent lavender-to-purple; Chinese Song Dynasty; colloidal silica iridescence

Firing effects:
- Yohen (窯変) / Kiln Transformation — unpredictable glaze color change; highly prized for uniqueness
- Yakishime (焼締) / Unglazed High-Fire — no glaze; raw clay body; Bizen / Shigaraki / Iga signature
- Hi-iro (緋色) / Flame Flash — brilliant orange-red marks from direct wood-flame contact; Bizen and Shigaraki
- Goma (胡麻) / Sesame Ash — natural ash deposits appearing as sesame-seed spots; Bizen specialty
- Botamochi (牡丹餅) / Ash Blob — large pooled ash accumulation like rice cake; Bizen specialty
- Hai-kaburi (灰被り) / Natural Ash Glaze — falling ash melts onto surface in wood kiln; Shigaraki / Iga / Tokoname
- Hi-tasuku (緋襷) / Cord Mark — orange-red lines from rice straw wrapped before firing; Bizen
- Koge (焦げ) / Scorching — carbon-black marks from incomplete combustion; Bizen / Iga / Shigaraki
- Tanadana-ato (棚板跡) / Kiln Shelf Mark — mark on foot from kiln shelf; not a defect in unglazed ware

-- TECHNIQUE DICTIONARY (22 techniques) --
- Sometsuke (染付) — Cobalt-blue underglaze on white porcelain; most ubiquitous Japanese porcelain decoration
- Uwaki-tsuke (上絵付) — Overglaze enamel at low temperature; enables full color palette including reds
- Shita-etsuke (下絵付) — Underglaze painting on unfired or bisque clay; limited to heat-stable pigments
- Kinsai (金彩) — Gold overglaze painting; common in Imari / Kutani / Satsuma export wares
- Ginsai (銀彩) — Silver overglaze; rarer than gold; prone to tarnish over time
- Tetsue (鉄絵) — Iron-oxide brushwork underglaze decoration; dark brown or black; E-Karatsu / Mino E-Shino
- Yurikou (釉裏紅) — Underglaze copper-red under transparent glaze; very difficult to control; Chinese origin
- Neriage (練り込み) — Multiple-colored clay bodies revealing marbled patterns when cut; no glaze decoration needed
- Mishima (三島) — Stamped pattern impressed into clay; white slip inlaid; Korean Goryeo origin
- Kohiki (粉引) — Dark clay dipped in white slip with translucent glaze; layered milky depth; Korean origin
- Tobikanna (飛び鉋) — Spring-steel chattering tool marks on rotating leather-hard clay; Onta and Mashiko
- Hakeme (刷毛目) — Bold brush-mark white slip over dark clay; Korean origin technique
- Sukashibori (透かし彫り) — Openwork carving creating open lattice; Mikawachi / Hirado specialty
- Nagashi-gake (流し掛け) — Trailed glaze in free unpredictable flows; Tamba and Iga specialty
- Inka-mon (印花文) — Repeated pattern impressed by stamping tool into clay
- Senkoku (線刻) — Design scratched into clay with pointed tool; visible under translucent glazes
- Takabori (高肉彫) — High relief carving from clay body or applied clay
- Ukibori (浮彫) — Low-to-medium relief carving; dragon / floral / geometric designs
- Rokuro-seikei (轆轤成形) — Standard wheel-thrown form; symmetrical circular cross-section
- Kakiemon style — Asymmetric motifs on milky nigoshide porcelain; overglaze red / blue / yellow
- Nabeshima style — Formal geometric underglaze and overglaze on refined porcelain; shogunate-era
- Satsuma moriage — Raised slip-paste decoration; Kyoto Satsuma export ware specialty
